"If you use AI to evaluate us, you're likelier to buy."
That's HubSpot's single biggest predictor of purchase intent right now.
Aja Frost came on the podcast and opened up their category-leading GEO playbook. We went deeper, taking a 201-to-301-level spin on best practices.
She shared several insights that are worth digging into further.
First, that HubSpot's primary KPI isn't citations. It's visibility, how often LLMs reference you and what they actually say. Citations drive some traffic, but most people never click them.
Second, they reorganized their 40-person team around products, not sections of the website. Every person owns the LLM visibility of a product and the demand it generates.
The messy middle (which page, which property) disappears when nobody's measured on their section's traffic anymore.
Third, their affiliate model now prices a citation directly. A new senior director built a methodology for what a single citation is worth to HubSpot, then plugged it into their existing affiliate bid strategy. Most programs still pay on last-click conversions and quietly undervalue the content shaping the LLM.
And a final line that stood out: B2B2C is becoming B2B2Bot. Your primary reader, especially in B2B, is often the agent.
I previously shared one of Aja's insights from this conversation around how TOFU is also "back" and a possible contributor to brand mentions, if not traffic that's worth monitoring. That clip is shown below, and one of my biggest takeaways and strategic shifts from the conversation.
Overall, I always find HubSpot is industry-leading from a tactics point of view, so it was a delight to have Aja Frost on to talk GEO strategy.

B2C content marketing does not drive many direct LLM clicks in 2026.
That doesn't mean it can't have value for other things, but in our analysis of 61 brands' content-type traffic from LLMs, traffic to editorial sections for B2C brands was only 7%.
On the B2B side, it was a completely different story: editorial/blog sections were more than a quarter of the clicks.
The likely reason? In B2B, "BOFU content" is a thing. Alternative and "Best X" pages are more prolific and performative in a way that they aren't in B2C.
Those conversion-focused pages get cited more in LLMs, all things equal, and thus are more likely to get clicks as a byproduct, too.
And there's also the unlisted influence these have on homepage clicks.
In B2C, those same page types are often covered by affiliate sites or high-authority publishers, in a way that just isn't as prolific within business circles.
The strategy indication for B2C? That influence in LLMs comes from other factors like affiliate sites and product pages.
Helping better optimize those pages, increasing the authority of your site, and driving mentions within affiliate listings is how you drive LLM influence instead.
They have impact in B2B as well, just not at the same level. Each vertical has a different scale, and strategy should adjust accordingly for that difference.
