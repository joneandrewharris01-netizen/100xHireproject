The AI glow isn't a quality problem. It's a perfection problem.
I was generating property images for a real estate workflow yesterday. First pass came out looking like a painting rather than a picture. Detailed prompt, good model, still off.
The fix wasn't more detail. It was imperfection.
I went back into Claude and asked for things that shouldn't be there in a "perfect" shot:
- Leaves fallen scattered on the terrace
- Faint watermarks on the limestone paving
- Signs of earlier rain
Second pass passed as real.
The mental shift: stop asking for the dream version of the scene. Tell the model "I want an image that could pass as real" and then give it the specific small things that would actually be there on a normal day.
I was against AI images on social a few months ago. Too cringey. Now my Meta ads creative and YouTube thumbnails run on this stack (Claude Desktop writes the prompt, OpenAI API generates the image, 5 to 50 cents a pop).
Worth testing if you've been burned by the AI glow.

Astro ships cleaner semantic HTML than 90% of WordPress themes. Schema markup is a 2-line prompt.

Find me a plugin-stacked WP site doing that.
"What about the client who wants to edit content?"
Three options. Markdown in a Git repo if they're technical.

Or teach them to use Claude Code directly. Yes, really. Non-technical people describe the change in plain English and it ships. That's the whole point.
"What about bookings, CRM, analytics?"
Every modern SaaS ships a script tag.
Calendly, HubSpot, PostHog, Plausible. Paste in, go. Anything fancier has an API. Never was a WordPress advantage.

Static sites with semantic HTML already outscore most plugin-heavy WP stacks. The WP accessibility story is mostly held together by one WCAG plugin that half the themes don't load correctly.

Agreed.
WordPress still wins one category: high-frequency, multi-author, non-technical editing at scale. News sites.
Blogs with 10 staff writers. Keep it there.

A sub-agent roleplays as someone who just googled your keyword. It reads what's out there and asks: "what am I STILL frustrated about? What didn't these articles answer?"
Think of it as hiring a really picky reader who tells you exactly where the existing content falls short.
Then a research agent goes hunting. YouTube tutorials, Reddit threads, Twitter discussions. The places where people share what they actually learned, not what ranks on Google.
The result?

The kind of thing I usually only hear at conferences from people who actually scale ad budgets.
