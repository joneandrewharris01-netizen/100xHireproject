C-suites want their teams to do cool shit with AI. But most aren't creating the space for their teams to actually go deep on it.
That was my biggest takeaway from yesterday's mastermind dinner in San Francisco.
I was telling the group about how one of my teammates isn't coming up with creative AI ideas for his work that seem obvious and surface-level to me.
That's when Ashley Faus cut in:
"But you think about AI all the time, right? And how much time does your teammate actually have for it? I bet he's buried in day-to-day work and barely has time to step back, look at the big picture, and come up with cool ideas of using AI."
I couldn't help but agree. That was exactly what was happening.
If I wanted a creative breakthrough with AI from my team, I should've slowed down the day-to-day and carved out dedicated time for them to explore.
Now, one might argue that truly ambitious people find the time. They'll dedicate evenings and weekends to learning new tools. And that's true... to a degree. But you can't expect everyone on your team to be obsessed with work and work alone. People want to live their lives. They want their spare time for things outside of work. And we should respect that.
So if you feel your team isn't innovating enough with AI — check whether you're giving them breathing room to experiment.
Chances are, you're the culprit. Not them.

9 out of 10 marketers have never vibe-coded an app with AI. And I think I know why.
On LinkedIn, it feels like everyone is about to colonize Mars with Claude Code. But when you talk to people face to face, most don't even know what "vibe coding" means.
So why are so many marketers still waiting to try AI?
I see four reasons:
1. Access.
AI tools cost money. Not everyone wants to spend their own cash to experiment while their company takes months to approve a Claude or ChatGPT subscription. So they just... wait.
2. Skepticism.
Some people don't believe AI is good enough yet. They tried it once six months ago, got a mediocre answer, and wrote it off.
3. The imagination gap.
Some people have access to cutting-edge AI agents but zero idea what to ask for. It's like writer's block, but for AI. They still think of it as a fancy search engine that browses the web and summarizes stuff.
4. Technical friction.
Even if you're sold on AI, building something real with a tool like Claude Code means dealing with a terminal, hosting, deployments. That's quite intimidating, since most marketers aren’t technical.
So here's my advice if you're just getting started:
Don't jump straight to the advanced tools like Claude Code or Codex. Ease in with something friendlier first. There are plenty of tools now that strip away most of the friction I just listed.
